This semester I got a freelance job for the first time in my life. Ive worked before but never related with coding or engineering. This months were tough, I learnt some valuable lessons, gained experience, felt overwhelmed, felt on top of the world and even felt like quitting. However, most of the times, I felt super hyped for the next day, hyped for shiping and building. 

My job started off as something pretty silly and short term at first, just a small web site where users can read digital magazines. As time went by, the project got more and more complex, from front to backend.
We ended up having users and linking automated email sending, integrated unique payments and subscriptions in two different payment platforms, and admin page with login, and even having a small mini game inside the page.

Of course, it still is a small, manageable project, but this being my first time working with other humans and having to reach their expectations while still having to attend uni, was hard.
Im studying software engineering at ITBA, in the second semester of my second year. I still have many tough subjects I have to face, so styudying for them is something my life demands. I had to make choices and learn to manage my time as effective as ever.

Im not perfect at managing time. Id say Im pretty bad still, but Im getting better. For sure, this experience made me realize some key points to have in mind while organizing time. I also had a first glance at working with others, and receiving orders and dealing with them. Here are two lessons/realizations I got.

First realization: *There are two types of responsibility, the one you have for ourself, and the one others demand from you.*

I learnt this shockingly fast in the first few weeks since I started working. I realized that I felt more pressure on having to ship and work rather than having to study, and I knew why. When you study for a test, you want to pass and get the best grade possible, we all do this, it is commitment with ourselves. When we dont do well on a test, we are the ones frustrated and the ones to blame too. It is our input, and our output (eventhough sometimes we can have bad luck), we get what we diserve. No matter if we ussully try to get the best, we may not ussualy grind for it. We take full responsibility for what happens then.

When you are working for someone else, and you receive tasks, opinions, judgment, and most importantly, expectations, your mind shifts (at least this happens to me). You understand that you craft now affects the craft of others, and other are dependent on you, on you knowledge, you communication, your ideas, your will. This challenged me deeply, in a more philosophical perspective. I started to give more importance to the work I did that affected others, rather than the one that affected me. I understood that if I failed, I was the one that had to carry the pain of test, but if I didnt do what I was told, or did it poorly, my companions where the ones to carry that pain. *Work done on behalf of others felt more significant than work done for my own benefit*. So I started to focus on that premise, and attended less classes and really grind the project. 

I know this conclussion might be related to this being my first job, but also It might have to do with the type of person I am, I have this tendency of leaving myself for later if its needed. 

Second realization: *50% of the job is done if it is well defined in the beggining.*

I was told this by a mentor I have before starting the job, I took the advice and try to apply it as I could. I didnt really know how imporant it was. When I started the job, I thought that everything was well defined, I wrote a docs with everything my boss wanted, dos and donts, as specific as I could. But I could have done better, much better. Its not only defining well what your boss tells you, but also you telling your boss where your job ends.

I had many calls and meetings where things changed and I had to re adapt or even delete code that fitted perfeclty for the first iteration of the product. I had to change things from week to week because my boss changed its mind. Im not blaming her, I know its part of working and life, Im blaming me, for not expecting it. This readaptations and new considerations forced me to change my calendar and rearrange my times. I even had to adapt the price at one point, due to the growth the project had. My advice here is to be very clear with what you understand that your user/boss wants, and to be very clear with what you will do. This will save you lots of precious time and useless code.
